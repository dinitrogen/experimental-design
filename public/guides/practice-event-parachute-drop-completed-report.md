# Parachute Drop — Completed Report Form


## A. Statement of Problem

How does the payload mass (in grams) affect the drop time (in seconds) of a parachute falling from a height of 1.5 meters?

---

## B. Hypothesis

If the payload mass attached to a parachute increases, then the drop time will decrease, because a heavier payload produces a greater downward gravitational force, which will overcome the air resistance (drag) from the canopy more quickly and cause the parachute to fall faster.

---

## C. Variables

### Independent Variable (IV)

**Payload mass** — the total mass of binder clips attached to the parachute, measured in **grams (g)** using a **kitchen scale**.

| Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 |
|:-------:|:-------:|:-------:|
| 10 g (1 clip) | 20 g (2 clips) | 30 g (3 clips) |

### Dependent Variable (DV)

**Drop time** — the time it takes for the parachute to fall from the release point to the floor, measured in **seconds (sec)** using a **stopwatch**.

### Controlled Variables (CV)

| Controlled Variable | How It Is Controlled |
|:-------------------|:---------------------|
| Canopy size | Same 25 cm diameter circle cut from a plastic trash bag for every trial |
| String length | Same four 15 cm strings attached at equal spacing around the canopy for every trial |
| Drop height | Parachute released from the same 1.5 m height (measured with a meterstick from the floor to the bottom of the payload) for every trial |


---

## D. Materials

- 1 plastic trash bag (to cut canopy from)
- 4 pieces of string, each 15 cm long
- Scissors (1 pair)
- Tape (to attach strings to canopy)
- 3 small binder clips (~10 g each)
- 1 meterstick
- 1 stopwatch
- 1 kitchen scale
- 1 hole punch
- 1 calculator

## E. Procedure and Set-up Diagrams

### Procedure

1. Cut one circular canopy with a 25 cm diameter from the plastic trash bag using scissors.
2. Use the hole punch to punch 4 evenly spaced holes around the edge of the canopy.
3. Cut 4 pieces of string, each 15 cm long.
4. Thread one string through each hole and tape it securely to the canopy edge.
5. Gather the 4 loose string ends together and clip them with 1 binder clip (10 g). This is the payload.
6. Use the meterstick to mark a release height of 1.5 m on a wall or door frame with a small piece of tape.
7. One team member holds the parachute by the top center of the canopy at the 1.5 m mark, with the payload hanging freely below.
8. A second team member holds the stopwatch ready.
9. The holder releases the parachute (no push, no spin). At the moment of release, the timer starts the stopwatch.
10. The timer stops the stopwatch when the payload touches the floor.
11. Record the drop time in the data table.
12. Repeat steps 7–11 two more times for a total of **3 trials at 10 g**.
13. Add a second binder clip to the payload (total: 2 clips, 20 g). Verify mass on the kitchen scale.
14. Repeat steps 7–11 for **3 trials at 20 g**.
15. Add a third binder clip to the payload (total: 3 clips, 30 g). Verify mass on the kitchen scale.
16. Repeat steps 7–11 for **3 trials at 30 g**.
17. Clean up the work area, and return or dispose of used materials.

## F. Qualitative Observations

### Set-Up
- The canopy was easy to cut into a circle using the trash bag. We traced a 25 cm diameter circle first using a ruler and a marker to make it accurate.
- The hole punch worked well for making attachment points. We spaced the 4 holes evenly by folding the canopy in half twice and punching at each fold.
- Attaching the strings with tape was straightforward. We made sure the tape was pressed firmly so the strings wouldn't detach during drops.

### Procedure
- The parachute opened consistently on most drops. Occasionally the canopy folded on itself during release, but this happened rarely (once during the 20 g trials).
- Releasing without any push or spin took some practice. Our first attempt at 10 g had a slight toss, so we discarded that trial and redid it.
- Adding binder clips was simple — they clipped onto the gathered strings easily and stayed secure.
- Timing was the most challenging part. The drops happened quickly (all under 2 seconds), so reaction time on the stopwatch may have introduced small errors.

### Results
- The results matched our expectations: heavier payloads fell faster.
- The drop times for 10 g were noticeably longer than for 30 g — you could visually see the difference.
- The data was fairly consistent across trials. No obvious outliers were apparent.
- There appears to be a clear downward trend — as payload mass increased, drop time decreased.
- The parachute at 30 g barely had time to fully open before hitting the ground, which may explain why the effect starts to level off at higher masses.

## J. Possible Experimental Errors

### Error 1

| | |
|:--|:--|
| **Type** | **Random** |
| **Specific Error Identified** | Human reaction time on the stopwatch. The person timing the drop may start or stop the stopwatch slightly early or late due to reaction time delay (~0.1–0.2 sec). |
| **Effect on Results** | This would cause individual drop time measurements to be slightly higher or lower than the true value, varying randomly across trials. Since the total drop times are short (1–2 sec), a 0.1 sec reaction time error represents a 5–10% error. This random error is reflected in the standard deviations of 0.04–0.05 sec at each IV level. It did not appear to affect the overall trend since the error was random (sometimes high, sometimes low). |

### Error 2

| | |
|:--|:--|
| **Type** | **Procedural** |
| **Specific Error Identified** | Inconsistent canopy opening during release. Sometimes the canopy folded on itself or did not fully inflate before the parachute hit the ground, especially at higher payload masses (30 g) where the fall was very fast. |
| **Effect on Results** | When the canopy did not fully open, the parachute experienced less air resistance than expected, causing the drop time to be shorter than the "true" value. This error would make the drop times at 30 g appear even shorter than they should be, potentially exaggerating the negative trend. This could explain why the drop time difference between 20 g and 30 g (0.20 sec) is slightly less than between 10 g and 20 g (0.30 sec). |

## K. Analysis of Claim/Evidence/Reasoning

### Data Trend

**Claim:** There is a negative correlation between payload mass and parachute drop time.

**Evidence:** The line of best fit for the mean drop time vs. payload mass is $y = -0.025x + 1.77$. The mean drop time decreased from 1.52 sec at 10 g, to 1.22 sec at 20 g, to 1.02 sec at 30 g.

**Reasoning:** Since the slope of the line of best fit is −0.025, which is negative, the drop time decreases as payload mass increases. For every additional gram of payload mass, the drop time decreases by approximately 0.025 seconds. Therefore, there is a negative correlation between payload mass and drop time — heavier payloads cause the parachute to fall faster.

### Variation *(State/Nationals only)*

**Claim:** The variation of the data remained approximately constant across all IV levels.

**Evidence:** The standard deviation for 10 g is 0.05 sec, for 20 g is 0.04 sec, and for 30 g is 0.05 sec. The IQR values are similarly consistent: 0.11, 0.10, and 0.13 sec.

**Reasoning:** The standard deviations are nearly identical across all three payload masses (0.05, 0.04, 0.05 sec), showing no clear increasing or decreasing pattern. Since standard deviation measures how far individual data points are spread from the mean, these similar values indicate the data was equally consistent at every IV level. This means that the payload mass did not affect the reliability of the measurements — the small variation was likely caused by random factors like stopwatch reaction time, not by the IV itself.

## L. Conclusion

**Hypothesis Restated:** If the payload mass attached to a parachute increases, then the drop time will decrease, because a heavier payload produces a greater downward gravitational force, which will overcome the air resistance (drag) from the canopy more quickly and cause the parachute to fall faster.

**Claim:** The hypothesis was supported. As the payload mass increased, the drop time decreased.

**Evidence:** The mean drop time decreased from 1.52 sec (10 g) to 1.22 sec (20 g) to 1.02 sec (30 g). The line of best fit is $y = -0.025x + 1.77$, with a negative slope of −0.025. There were no outliers in the data, and the standard deviations were consistent (0.04–0.05 sec), indicating reliable measurements.

**Reasoning:** The data clearly supports the hypothesis. As payload mass increased by 10 g at each level, the mean drop time consistently decreased. This makes physical sense: the gravitational force on the payload equals $F = mg$, so a heavier payload experiences a larger downward force. Since the canopy size was held constant (25 cm diameter), the upward drag force remained the same. The net downward force therefore increases with mass, causing the parachute system to accelerate faster and reach a higher terminal velocity, resulting in a shorter drop time. Based on these results, the aerospace engineering team should keep the payload mass as low as possible to maximize drop time and ensure a safe, slow descent for supply packages.

## M. Improvements, Applications & Recommendations for Further Use

### Improvements for the Current Experiment

1. **Use a photosensor or video analysis** instead of a handheld stopwatch to measure drop time. This would eliminate human reaction time error (~0.1–0.2 sec), which is significant when total drop times are only 1–2 seconds.
2. **Increase the drop height to 2.5–3 meters** so the parachute has more time to fully open and reach a steady falling speed. At 1.5 m, especially with 30 g, the parachute barely opened before hitting the ground.
3. **Increase the number of trials to 5 per IV level** to reduce the impact of random error and produce more reliable mean values and more meaningful standard deviations.

### Practical Applications

1. **Humanitarian aid drops:** Understanding how payload mass affects fall time helps organizations design parachutes for airdropping food, medicine, and supplies to disaster zones — ensuring packages land slowly enough not to be damaged.
2. **Drone delivery systems:** Companies designing drone-deployed parachute packages can use this relationship to select the right parachute size for a given package weight.
3. **Aerospace safety (ejection seats/capsule recovery):** Engineers designing emergency parachute systems for aircraft ejection seats or spacecraft recovery capsules must account for the mass of the person or capsule to ensure safe deceleration.

### Future Experiments

1. **Vary canopy diameter** (10 cm, 20 cm, 30 cm, 40 cm) while keeping payload constant — to determine the optimal canopy size for maximum drop time.
2. **Vary canopy shape** (circle, square, hexagon) with equal surface areas to investigate whether shape affects drag efficiency.
3. **Vary canopy material** (trash bag, tissue paper, fabric) to investigate how material porosity or weight affects drop time.
